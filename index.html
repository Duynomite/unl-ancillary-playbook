Module 1 · Section 1 · Conviction

Why this matters

This is the conviction layer. Internalize it before the mechanics.

Every MA customer you talk to has a real need for HI/HHC, and almost none of them know it. This isn't sales-speak — it's structural to the product profile:

  1. 1. They almost all have a high hospital copay they're exposed to. The vast majority of MA plans charge $200–450/day for an inpatient stay. The average 4-day admission lands a $1,200–1,800 surprise bill. Almost no MA customer has thought about this. None of them have coverage for it.
  2. 2. They almost all take prescriptions — and HHC's built-in Rx reimbursement pays them cash they can apply against their overall coverage spending. A customer filling 4 medications/month receives ~$40–50/month from the HHC Rx reimbursement, a policy feature of HHC paid to the customer regardless of any other coverage they have.
  3. 3. After a hospital stay, daily-living help is the predictable next chapter. Falls, strokes, hip replacements, cardiac events — Medicare covers the hospital and the rehab facility (kind of), but the moment they go home and need help bathing, dressing, getting up, eating? Medicare doesn't pay for that. None of their plans pay for that. Family steps in (or doesn't).
  4. 4. Medicare leaves the gap by design. HI/HHC was built to fill exactly this gap. Original Medicare, Medigap, AND Medicare Advantage all cover medical home care almost identically — and none of them cover daily-living help. The product wasn't built for "some seniors who might need this." It was built for the Medicare customer profile. Your prospect IS the target customer.

HHC Plan B premium alone: ~$53/mo. After the customer applies their ~$50/mo Rx reimbursement, net spend on the HHC side is around ~$3–5/mo.

HI Shield premium alone: ~$40/mo. HI has no built-in Rx reimbursement (the Rx feature belongs to HHC only).

Customer who chooses to enroll in both: ~$93/mo combined premium. Their Rx reimbursement (~$50/mo) reduces total out-of-pocket spending to roughly $43/mo across both policies. The Rx reimbursement is a policy feature of HHC — not a discount or rebate on HI. The customer receives that cash whether or not they also enroll in HI. The practical effect on someone who enrolls in both: net spending is dramatically lower than the headline premiums suggest.

Default goal on every call is to surface both HI and HHC — they cover complementary exposures (hospital admission vs. home recovery + Rx). Each product is independently underwritten, independently priced, and independently enrolled. The customer's decision on each is fully independent — they can buy HI alone, HHC alone, both, or neither. Your job is to surface both so the customer can make an informed decision on each.

When you don't surface HI/HHC on an MA call, you're letting your customer walk away with an unaddressed exposure they would thank you for surfacing. You're not pitching. You're advising. They have real problems they don't know about, and you have a package solution that costs them about $43/month — roughly $1.50/day — for hospital cash on admission, home healthcare for the recovery, and a $3,500 caregiver benefit.

Step into their shoes

★ The Robert vignette — read it slowly

Picture Robert. He's 70, retired carpenter in North Carolina. Wife Linda is home with him. Two kids — one in Atlanta, one near Asheville. He's on Social Security — call it $1,900/month. He takes 4 medications. He just enrolled in his MA plan with you and feels good about it.

Now picture this: 11 months from now, Robert slips on his back deck. He's admitted for 4 days. The hospital bill arrives 30 days later: $1,580. That's 83% of his monthly income, gone in one envelope. He's also weak coming home and needs help getting around for 6 weeks — bathing, getting dressed, walking to the bathroom. Linda tries but she has her own arthritis. His daughter drives 4 hours from Atlanta, takes 2 weeks off work to help. They draw down savings he was hoping to leave to his grandkids.

Question for you: if you were Robert, would you have wanted someone to tell you, 11 months earlier, that for about $43/month you could have had:

  • ~$1,200 in cash arrive to cover that hospital bill (from HI Shield)
  • ~$300/day in cash for the home healthcare he needed for 6 weeks afterward (from HHC Shield)
  • $3,500 directly to his daughter for her 2 weeks of caregiving (from TCare)
  • Prescription reimbursement every month that funded most of the cost of the policies in the first place

Of course he would. He'd thank you. His daughter would thank you. That's the customer you're talking to on every MA call right now. They don't know what they don't know. The only person in their life likely to surface these exposures is the Medicare agent. That's you. And you'd be selling them the package — HI for hospital cash + HHC for home care + Rx reimbursement that offsets the premium — for less than $50/month real cost.

The moral case (state it to yourself before every call)

Here's the line that defines master agents:

★ Memorize this

"Not surfacing HI/HHC isn't 'respecting their decision not to be sold to.' It's actively choosing to let them walk away with real exposures I could have prevented for about $43/month — roughly $1.50/day. Both are choices. I'll make the right one."

The default agent posture is "I don't want to bother them with another product." That sounds polite. It's actually negligence dressed up as politeness. Robert didn't tell you not to mention the hospital copay. You decided not to. And his $1,580 bill in month 11 is the consequence of YOUR decision, not his.

This isn't guilt-tripping. It's the real moral architecture of the job. You're not a salesperson interrupting their day. You're an advisor who has insight they need. Acting on that insight is the job. Skipping it is failing the job.

This is the conviction. Internalize it. It's what gets you to attempt the cross-sell on call #47 when you're tired and the prospect sounds uninterested — because you know they need this, the math proves it, and skipping it is the worse choice for both of you.

Module 1 · Section 2 · Mental model + Discipline

The two-step motion + the discipline that makes it pay

The mental model is simple. Two steps. Run them on every call.

★ The whole motion in two sentences

Step 1. Take the MA lead. Do your normal job. See if you can sell MA.

Step 2. Whether MA sold or not, surface BOTH HI and HHC as two separate supplemental products that work alongside each other.

That's it. Two steps. The default goal on Step 2 is to surface both products — they cover complementary exposures (HI = cash on hospital admission; HHC = cash for daily-living help + prescription reimbursement feature + caregiver lump). Selling one without surfacing the other usually leaves either a hospital gap or a home-care gap on the table that the customer would benefit from understanding.

Each product is independently underwritten, independently priced, and independently enrolled. The customer can choose to enroll in HI alone, HHC alone, both, or neither — the decision on each is fully independent. Your job is to surface both so the customer can make an informed decision on each. Never imply they're required-together, bundled, required-for-Medicare, or that one is conditional on the other.

When a customer can't qualify for one (mid-call HI knockout, Medigap making HI redundant, etc.), present the one they qualify for on its own merits. The default sales motion is to surface both; the customer's enrollment decision on each is independent.

The product fits the customer profile by design, so the question isn't "do they fit?" — it's "will I help them see the fit?" If you don't surface both on Step 2, you've left a real exposure on the table for your customer that they would have benefited from understanding. That's the outcome to avoid.

The NEPQ application — surface the problem they don't know they have

NEPQ's core insight: people don't act on problems they don't recognize. Your job in Step 2 isn't to PITCH — it's to help the prospect SEE an exposure they're already carrying. The product sells itself once they see it.

Three Problem Awareness questions do almost all the work. Drill these:

"If you got admitted to the hospital next month, do you know what your plan's copay would be per day?"
Why it works: Most won't know. Some will guess low. The real answer — $200–450/day — lands as a small shock.
"Have you thought about what happens after a hospital stay — say a fall or a stroke — when you're home but you need help bathing, dressing, getting up? Medicare doesn't cover that. Who would help you?"
Why it works: Forces them to imagine the scenario. Then to imagine the helper. Then to feel the gap.
"How would a surprise $1,500 hospital bill feel right now?"
Why it works: Concrete number. Real exposure. Forces them to feel it, not just think about it.

After they articulate the problem, you don't have to sell — they're already looking for the solution. The Rx-Net-Cost Reframe (Section 8) is the close because by the time you reveal that the HHC side nets to $3–5/month and the full HI + HHC package is about $43/month — roughly $1.50/day — for hospital cash + home healthcare + caregiver lump, the customer's internal voice is "that's a no-brainer."

The master-teacher principle

The mechanics in the rest of this guide (the script integrations, the close sequence, the objection handling) all serve ONE thing: helping you run Step 2 effectively on every call. Don't get lost in the mechanics. Stay anchored to the conviction (Section 1) and the two-step motion (this one). If you find yourself feeling salesy, you've drifted from advising. The fix is always to go back to a Problem Awareness question and let the prospect articulate the gap.

Two mantras to memorize

  1. 1. "Cash clarity beats feature overload." $300/day in your pocket if you're admitted lands. Daily Hospital Confinement Indemnity Benefit doesn't.
  2. 2. "You're not selling fear. You're offering options, clarity, and control." Surface the gap; never weaponize it. This is the difference between advising and pitching.

The voice that says "skip this one"

Every cross-sell-trained agent hears the same internal voice at some point in a call. It usually sounds polite and reasonable:

  • • "They sound tired — I don't want to push it."
  • • "They're in a hurry — I'll skip it this time."
  • • "They already pushed back on the MA close — they're not going to be receptive."
  • • "They've got Medigap — probably not a fit."
  • • "I've already been on the call 35 minutes — let's wrap."

Every one of those is the same thought wearing different clothes: "I don't feel like attempting the cross-sell on this call." That's fine as a feeling. It's not fine as a decision.

You don't decide who needs HI/HHC. Every MA customer is the target customer (Section 1). Your only decision is whether you'll help them see the fit. Skipping = deciding for them, without consulting them, that they don't need it. That's not your call to make.

How master agents override the voice

Three moves the top closers use to stay consistent:

  1. 1. Anchor to the moral case. Before each cross-sell transition, say to yourself: "If I don't surface this, I'm choosing to let them walk away with the exposure." That sentence neutralizes the "skip this one" voice in two seconds.
  2. 2. Lower the bar from "close" to "attempt." You're not deciding whether to close. You're deciding whether to ASK the Problem Awareness questions (Section 2). The asking is the discipline. The close is downstream of the asking. Most agents conflate them and skip the asking because they're not sure they can close.
  3. 3. Use the conviction, not the script, when you're tired. Script energy is high-effort to fake when you're depleted. Conviction is low-effort because it's just stating what you believe. When you're tired on call #47, don't try to "perform" the cross-sell — just say what you actually believe: "Hey, before we wrap, your plan has a $300/day hospital copay you're exposed to. Want to spend two minutes on that?" Conviction in plain words beats a polished script delivered with no energy.

Module 1 · Section 3 · Product depth

Know your products — depth + tier-matching

You'll sell this with conviction or you won't sell it at all. The agents who close the most cross-sells aren't the ones who memorized the most features — they're the ones who can picture EXACTLY what their customer will go through if they need this, and can describe it like they've watched it happen.

The Medicare Gap — what Medicare actually covers (and doesn't)

Most agents — and almost every customer — think Medicare covers more than it does. The gap is structural and intentional. Get this in your bones.

What Original Medicare, Medicare Advantage, and Medigap all cover the same way (medical home care):

  • • Nursing visits (skilled care)
  • • Physical therapy, occupational therapy, speech therapy
  • • Wound care, IV therapy
  • • Medical equipment
⚠ The catch — Medicare home health is $0-cost ONLY if ALL 7 conditions hold
  • 1. Care is "skilled" (nursing or therapy)
  • 2. Customer is "homebound" (leaving home requires considerable effort)
  • 3. Care is "intermittent" (not 24-hour)
  • 4. Provided by a Medicare-certified agency
  • 5. In the customer's MA plan network (if MA)
  • 6. Prior authorization obtained (if required)
  • 7. Under an active plan of care from a doctor

Miss ANY one of these → 100% patient pay. And these conditions reset every 60 days.

What NONE of them cover — the gap HHC fills:

  • • Daily-living help — bathing, dressing, toileting, transferring, eating, continence
  • • 24-hour custodial care — when someone needs continuous supervision (dementia)
  • • Help that isn't "skilled" — meal prep, laundry, light cleaning, medication reminders
  • • Indefinite-duration care — Medicare home health is short-term recovery; nobody pays for the long term
Many customers will tell you "Medicare covers 100 days of home care." They're confusing it with the Skilled Nursing Facility rule. Medicare home health has NO time limit — but it only covers skilled care under the 7 conditions above. The 100-day SNF rule is for post-hospital nursing home stays. Two different things; both leave the daily-living gap.
Some MA plans advertise "in-home support" benefits. Only ~7% of MA plans actually offer meaningful in-home help (KFF 2026 data), and when they do it's usually capped at small dollar amounts and limited to chronically ill members. Always offer to walk the customer through their Summary of Benefits — if their MA plan does cover something, great. The home care coverage we're discussing pays cash directly regardless of what their MA does.

The family caregiving reality

When the agent asks during NEADS "Who would step in to help if you needed daily care for a few months?" — the customer pictures their daughter, son, spouse, niece. They don't picture the actual financial and emotional weight of what they're asking. The agent should understand it for them.

The numbers (AARP 2024 data):

  • • $470 billion/year in unpaid family caregiving labor in the U.S.
  • • Average family caregiver loses $7,200/yr in wages and spends another $7,200/yr out-of-pocket for the person they're caring for
  • • 28% of family caregivers reduce work hours; 23% leave jobs entirely; many never fully recover their career trajectory

Family doesn't "help" — they sacrifice. Daughters drive 4 hours from another state. Sons take FMLA leave. Spouses stop sleeping. The $3,500 TCare benefit doesn't make them whole, but it tells them "someone is footing some of the bill."

When you understand this, the family-burden discovery question lands different. You're not running a sales tactic — you're surfacing a real exposure the customer and their family carry that nobody else in their life is going to mention.

  • • Rx reimbursement of $10/generic + $25/brand every fill, up to $600/yr cap (from HHC)

Don't deliver knockouts like a checklist. Weave them into natural Rx and family discovery. Example:

Mid-call DQ — graceful break (when an HI knockout fires)

What happens at claim time — the customer's experience

Understanding claim flow makes you a better pitcher. When a prospect asks "so how does it actually work when I need it?" — most agents wave their hand. The agents who close walk through it like they've done it before. Here's what to know.

HI Shield — hospital admission claim

  1. 1. Customer is admitted to the hospital. The hospital handles billing through Medicare/their MA plan as normal — this is separate from HI.
  2. 2. Within 30 days of discharge, the customer (or family member) calls UNL's claims line to report the admission. They provide: admission date, discharge date, hospital name, attending physician.
  3. 3. UNL confirms the admission was inpatient (not observation) and that any waiting period/pre-existing conditions have cleared.
  4. 4. UNL mails a check to the customer for $X per day × days admitted. (Example: $300/day × 4 days = $1,200 check.)
  5. 5. The cash is the customer's — no restriction. They can apply it to the hospital bill, household expenses, anything.
  6. 6. Typical processing time: 2–4 weeks from claim filing to check delivery.

HHC Shield — home healthcare claim

  1. 1. The trigger fires: either (a) the customer can't perform 2 of 6 ADLs and a doctor certifies it, OR (b) the customer has cognitive impairment certified by a doctor.
  2. 2. The customer (or family member) calls UNL's claims line and reports the qualifying event. UNL conducts an assessment (typically over the phone or via a brief in-home visit, depending on situation).
  3. 3. Once UNL certifies the qualifying condition, HHC starts paying.
  4. 4. Monthly checks mailed to the customer at the daily benefit rate, for as long as the customer remains in qualifying need, up to 360 days.
  5. 5. Periodic re-certification may be required to confirm continued need.
  6. 6. The cash is the customer's — no restriction on how it's spent.

TCare — caregiver benefit (Plan B/C only)

  1. 1. While the customer is receiving HHC benefits, a family member can step in to help.
  2. 2. That caregiver registers with TCare separately. Completes a brief assessment that confirms they're providing care.
  3. 3. TCare pays $3,500 directly to the caregiver — not to the customer. This is the caregiver's check.
  4. 4. Up to two lifetime TCare payouts per policy ($7,000 max).

Rx reimbursement — every prescription fill (HHC only)

  1. 1. Customer fills a prescription at any pharmacy, paying whatever their MA copay is (could be $0).
  2. 2. Customer emails the pharmacy receipt to UNL at claims@unlinsurance.com. Each email must include: pharmacy name, drug names, insured name, date, and RX code. (Or calls UNL Agent Service at 800-635-1993 for help.)
  3. 3. UNL processes the receipt. Mails the customer a check: $10 per generic fill, $25 per brand-name fill, up to the annual cap for their tier (A: $300, B: $600, C: $900).
  4. 4. Typical processing time: 2–3 weeks from submission to check.
  5. 5. Customer can submit individual receipts as they fill, or batch them monthly — both work.

How the Rx reimbursement actually works — the cash flow

This is the single most important compliance + persistency point in the whole guide. Internalize it. Then teach it to every customer the same way.

The Rx reimbursement is NOT a discount on the premium. It's not a coupon. It doesn't reduce the bill. It's a separate cash payment the customer receives by mail after they submit pharmacy receipts.

The cash flow from the customer's perspective:

  1. 1. $53/month gets drafted automatically from the customer's bank account for the HHC Shield premium. This happens on a set day each month — just like any insurance bill. The full $53 leaves their account.
  2. 2. The customer fills prescriptions at their pharmacy — using their MA plan as they normally would. They pay whatever copay their MA plan charges (could be $0, could be a few dollars).
  3. 3. The customer emails the pharmacy receipt(s) to UNL at claims@unlinsurance.com. Each email must include: pharmacy name, drug names, insured name, date, and RX code. (Customer can also call UNL Agent Service at 800-635-1993.)
  4. 4. UNL processes the receipt and mails a check to the customer's home address. $10 for each generic, $25 for each brand name. Typical processing time: 2–3 weeks from when UNL receives the receipt.
  5. 5. Net cash flow over a typical month for a 4-medication customer: $53 out (premium draft) → $40–50 back (reimbursement check) → ~$3–5 net out-of-pocket.

Why this matters for compliance and persistency

If the customer thinks the policy "only costs $3/month" — they will be confused when $53 hits their bank account. They will call to complain. They will likely cancel. That's a refund, a chargeback, a regulatory complaint, and a damaged trust relationship — all from one bad explanation.

If the customer understands they're paying $53 monthly and receiving ~$50 back through a separate reimbursement check by submitting receipts — they'll set up the workflow, submit their receipts, get their checks, and stay enrolled. That's the persistency mechanic.

"Great. I'm going to walk you through the application right here on the line — takes about 10 minutes. I'll ask you the questions, you give me the answers, and at the end we'll do the signature. Sound good?"

Signature — two options (your choice based on situation)

UNL accepts signatures in two ways. Use the text option as the default; fall back to the 3-way call when the customer can't receive a text.

Option A — Text confirmation code (default)

UNL sends a text message with a confirmation code to the customer's phone. The customer reads the code back to you over the line. You enter the code to complete the signature. The text can go to the customer's phone OR to the phone of someone with them — useful if the customer doesn't have their phone handy, doesn't have texting, or is hard of hearing.

Verbatim setup:

"For the signature, I'm going to have UNL send a text with a confirmation code. The text can go to your phone, or to anyone's phone who's with you right now. Once it comes through, you'll just read me the code and we're done with the signature. Whose phone should I send it to?"

Option B — 3-way call with UNL (fallback)

When the customer has no one available to receive a text, 3-way call UNL for a verbal signature confirmation. Bring UNL onto the line, introduce the customer, and UNL handles the signature verification verbally on the recording.

Verbatim setup:

"No problem — we have another way to do this. I'm going to bring UNL on the line and they'll confirm the signature with you verbally. Hold on one moment while I dial them in."
Never proceed past signature without one of the two confirmation methods completed. The signature is what makes the application valid. If neither works on this call, schedule a callback to complete the signature step — but don't submit the application without it.

The Referral Ask (right between Q2 and starting the application)

After Q2 (they just told you why), BEFORE you start the application:

"While I have you — do you have a friend or family member who might benefit from coverage like this? No pressure, just thinking of who else this would be a fit for."
They're in "yes" mode. Decision fresh. They feel good about it. NOT during application (compliance flow). NOT after welcome call (momentum gone).

If they give a name: the referral has to call US — we can't outbound-call them without their consent (TCPA / one-to-one consent rule). Pass the introduction back through your customer.

"That's great. Here's how we have to handle it for compliance — they'd need to call us directly. I'll give you my direct number to pass along, and when they call in I'll take care of them just like I'm taking care of you. Sound good?"

The customer carries the introduction; the referral self-initiates contact. Same warm-lead value, zero TCPA exposure.

If they say no: "No problem at all." Move straight into the application. Don't push.

We cannot compliantly call a referral cold. Even with the original customer's permission, the referral hasn't given us consent — outbound calling them is a TCPA one-to-one consent violation ($500–$1,500 per call). The compliant path is to give the customer your number and have the referral call inbound.

The post-sale 60 seconds — chargeback prevention

After the signature is confirmed and the application is submitted, BEFORE ending the call. The 60 seconds after submission is where you protect the sale from a month-2-3 cancellation.

Cover these four things, in order:

  1. 1. Policy delivery: "You'll get the policy by mail or email in [timeframe]. From UNL Insurance."
  2. 2. First premium: "First payment on [date]. After that, same date each month."
  3. 3. Claims: "Number's on the policy. You can also call me back and I'll walk you through it."
  4. 4. ★ Welcome call (most important): "Within the next [X] days, you'll get a quick call from UNL just confirming you're satisfied. It's a normal verification call. Just say yes — they're checking to make sure I represented things correctly."
A surprised welcome call is the #1 cause of 30-day cancellations. Mention it so it feels expected, not random. Two sentences here save dozens of chargebacks per year.

The Rx setup walkthrough (post-sale, during the 60 seconds)

Most agents miss this. Walk the customer through the exact Rx submission flow before you end the call. This is what keeps Rx-funded HHC customers enrolled in month 2 and beyond.

"Three — for the prescription reimbursement to work, you'll email your pharmacy receipts to UNL at claims@unlinsurance.com. Include the pharmacy name, drug names, your name, date, and the RX code on each one. Save your receipts and submit them — usually monthly works best. You'll start receiving reimbursement checks within 2–3 weeks of submitting. If you have any questions, the UNL service number is 800-635-1993."

Three persistency-building language moves

People cancel policies they don't feel ownership of. These three small language patterns transfer ownership to the prospect — and dramatically reduce month 2–3 cancellations.

  1. 1. Frame the policy as "active starting [date]," not "your new policy." Specific date creates a mental anchor. They remember when coverage began.
  2. 2. Acknowledge the free-look period without highlighting it. "You'll have [X] days to review the policy when it arrives." Said once, in passing. Don't sell against it.
  3. 3. Mention the claims process casually as "when," not "if." "When you ever need to use this, you'll call [number]." Subtle, but pre-frames using the policy as inevitable, not hypothetical.
You're not selling them a policy; you're handing them something they already own.

Module 2 · Section 8 · Audio intelligence

Reading the customer — the audio intelligence beneath the script

A great script delivered by an agent who can't read the room is just noise. This section trains the underlying perception — tonality, pacing, the specific phrases that signal a buyer is leaning in or pulling back, and the internal state that produces the right delivery without performance. These skills apply throughout every call. Get them in your bones and you stop sounding like an agent and start sounding like an advisor.

Tonality matching — pull 10-15% below the prospect

At ages 65 to 85, average comprehension speed is roughly 30% slower than at 45. If you talk at your normal sales pace, the prospect is processing the first half of your sentence while you're already three sentences ahead. They're not slow — they're operating at normal age-cognition speed against unnatural agent pace.

Senior-call principle: always pace below the prospect, never above. Match their tempo at the start, then gently pull the whole conversation down 10–15%. They'll follow you without noticing, and they'll feel heard the entire way.

The prospect just said yes to MA. Your energy is up. Theirs may be cooling. Reset your pace before you say "last thing before we wrap up..." — otherwise the cross-sell sounds like a separate hard sell.

Three pacing techniques:

  • • Match-and-pace. Start at their tempo. Over the first 60 seconds, pull the conversation down 10–15%. They follow without noticing.
  • • The deliberate pause. A one-second pause after a question forces the prospect to fill it. Agents who fear silence kill their own discovery.
  • • Soften the stress. When you're about to say something the prospect might resist — price, knockout question, anything sensitive — drop your volume slightly. Senior prospects hear "louder" as "pushier."
⚠ The most common tonality mistake

Matching their nervous energy upward instead of pulling it down. If a prospect sounds anxious, don't match anxious — anchor calm. You're not mirroring their state; you're modeling the state you want them to settle into.

The calm-expert persona — internal state, not performance

Agents who try to perform calm sound performative. Agents who actually feel calm sound calm. The way to feel calm on call #47 is to know what's happening before the prospect speaks — and to hold the right internal posture before you open your mouth.

This section isn't about what calm sounds like. It's about what calm thinks like.

Before you speak, your internal posture is: I'm curious, not selling. I'm present, not performing. The prospect isn't an obstacle — they're someone who needs information I have. My job is to find out if this fits. If it doesn't, I'll DQ kindly. If it does, the math sells itself. Either way, I'm here to listen first.

When you hold that posture before you pick up the phone, the opener writes itself. The pace settles automatically. The prospect feels the difference in the first ten seconds — and you didn't have to perform a thing.

Three components of the calm-expert state:

  • Curiosity over conviction. Replace "I need to close this" with "I want to know if this fits." Curiosity-led tone is naturally calmer than conviction-led tone — and conviction-led tone is what prospects experience as pressure.
  • Presence over performance. Most agents are mentally one step ahead of the conversation — already scripting the next move while the prospect is still talking. Presence means listening with full attention, even on the hundredth call where you already know the answer.
  • Service over outcome. The prospect can tell whether you care about their fit or your sale. The way to actually care: assume each prospect might be a graceful DQ. Then when they're not, you've earned the close honestly.
Their mental state mirrors yours. If you're calm, they get calmer. If you're anxious, they get anxious. You don't control the prospect — but you do control which state you bring to the call, and that controls most of how they respond.

The mid-call self-check (run at minute 5)

At roughly the five-minute mark of every call, run a quick internal audit. Most agents drift without realizing it — these four questions catch the drift in about ten seconds and let you correct mid-call.

  1. 1. Am I still in discovery, or did I slip into pitching too early? If they haven't told me their Rx mix yet, I'm not ready to present. Go back.
  2. 2. Is my pace still below theirs? If I've sped up, I'm getting eager. Pull it back down a notch.
  3. 3. Have I asked an open question in the last 90 seconds? If not, I've slipped into monologue. Ask one now.
  4. 4. What's their energy compared to three minutes ago? Up = leaning in, keep going. Down = losing them, slow down and re-engage. Flat = ambivalent — pivot to a deeper question.

Module 2 · Section 9 · Discovery moves

NEPQ discovery mastery — the moves that make prospects sell themselves

NEPQ's core insight: people don't act on problems they don't recognize. Discovery isn't a checklist — it's a sequence of questions that surface a real exposure the prospect is already carrying. By the time you get to math, they've articulated four problems to themselves. You don't have to convince them. They already know.

The 4 NEPQ discovery seeds

Plant these four during NEADS. Each surfaces a real exposure the customer carries every day but has never explicitly thought about.

Seed 1 — The hospital-copay surface: "If you were admitted to the hospital next month, do you know what your plan's copay would be per day? On most MA plans, it's typically $200–400/day. That's $1,500–2,000 for a typical 4-day stay."
Seed 2 — The post-stay daily-living surface: "Have you ever needed help around the house — bathing, dressing, meals — even temporarily after an illness or surgery? Medicare doesn't cover that kind of care, by the way."
Seed 3 — The family-burden surface: "Do you have a daughter, son, or spouse who would step in to help if you needed daily care for a few months?"
Seed 4 — The past-experience surface: "Has anyone close to you — a parent, sibling, friend — been hospitalized or needed help at home in the last couple years? Do you know what they ended up paying out of pocket?"
These aren't random discovery — they're engineered NEPQ moves:
  • Q1 makes them feel the hospital copay risk (number is real, specific, unfamiliar)
  • Q2 makes them realize Medicare's daily-living gap (most don't know this)
  • Q3 forces them to picture the helper (family they'd burden, or no one at all)
  • Q4 turns their own observed experience into the teaching — people believe what they've seen more than what you assert

★ The Implication follow-up — the NEPQ master move

After any seed lands with concern, use the consequence follow-up:

"If something like that happened to you and you didn't have a way to cover it — how do you think it would play out?"

Forces them to mentally simulate the bad outcome. Prospects who simulate the consequence convert at 3-5x the rate of prospects who only acknowledge the abstract risk. This is the single highest-leverage NEPQ move you can add to discovery.

The Preference Question — the highest-leverage discovery move

Ask this AFTER the seeds + Rx discovery:

"If you had an accident or a surgery and later had to choose between recovering in a nursing facility or recovering at home with someone coming over to help you bathe, dress, and get around — which would you want?"

About 95% of seniors say "home." Why this specific wording works:

  1. A real, specific scenario — "accident or surgery" is concrete, plausible, not hypothetical-feeling.
  2. The "later" framing — moves them past the event into recovery, where the choice actually matters.
  3. Three specific activities — "bathe, dress, get around" makes them picture a real person doing real things. Not abstract "care." And those three are the actual HHC trigger ADLs — clever foreshadowing for the product you'll present next.

That one answer does three things at once:

  1. 1. Data — confirms home preference (the HHC sale)
  2. 2. Seed — they articulated the value proposition before you named the product
  3. 3. Anchor — in the presentation, you link back: "You said you'd want to recover at home — that's exactly what this is designed for."
After they say "home" (95% will), the NEPQ master move is to LOOP:

"Say more about that — what is it about home that matters to you?"

Then stay silent. Whatever they say next is THEIR reason — articulated in their own words. You'll anchor back to it in the close: "You said home matters to you because [their words] — that's exactly what this is designed to protect."

One loop turns a binary answer into a value-anchor that makes the close inevitable.

Looping doctrine — 6 to 12 moves per call

After the prospect says something important, loop it back:

"You said [their exact phrase] — say more about that?"

Why: Doubles trust (they feel heard). Forces elaboration (second articulation is more emotional than first). Surfaces the real concern under the surface concern. Buys you thinking time without sounding scripted.

6–12 loops per call. Gone 90 seconds without one? You're missing rapport.

Then stay silent after they answer. Don't add to their reason. Their words are stronger than yours could ever be.

Family-burden discovery (the HHC pre-frame)

Robert says his kids are spread out (Atlanta, Asheville). Patricia says her husband Mike is on disability. These details reshape the rest of the call.

After the Preference Question lands, ask:

"About 70% of folks who hit 65 end up needing that kind of help at some point. If it happened to you, who'd be helping?"

Then loop their answer. By the time you get to math, they've articulated the gap themselves — you never had to say "you need this."

Energy shift detection — the macro buying signal

Beyond individual buying phrases, watch for the bigger pattern. Three macro indicators that the prospect crossed from "interested" to "ready":

  1. 1. Energy shift — questions go from "what does it cover" to "what's next." They stopped evaluating; they started planning.
  2. 2. The "we" pronoun appearing — "If we had this..." / "We'd just need to..." They're already imagining themselves with the policy.
  3. 3. Theoretical → practical — "When would coverage start?" / "What does my wife need to do?" Questions get logistical.
Continuing to sell past the shift. Flat tone after engaged tone means you missed the close. Stop mid-sentence and ask "Where's your head at right now?"

Tells you'll hear mentions taking care of partner, partner's health conditions, may reference being tired or "stretched thin." Often has high health literacy because they navigate spouse's care.
Product fit Both products with particularly strong HHC fit. They already SEE what daily-living care looks like. Many will recognize themselves as future HHC claimants.
Knockouts to watch Their own health may be deteriorating from caregiver burnout — gently confirm none of the HI knockouts surfaced.
What resonates "What happens to the spouse if you can't care for them anymore?" — this isn't abstract for them. TCare benefit matters because they've seen the financial weight of unpaid caregiving firsthand. The "your own health while caring for someone" angle.
Expected close path Phase 6 with extra emphasis on HHC + TCare. Often the most receptive prospect type because they understand the gap intuitively. High close rate when fit is clean.
Watch out for They sometimes want to put the policy on the SPOUSE (the one with disability). Spouse may knock out on health. Clarify early which person is the proposed insured.
Profile 5
The Cancer Survivor

70–80yo, had cancer 2–10 years ago, in remission, takes a couple maintenance meds, generally cautious about health.

Tells you'll hear mentions cancer history during health questions, may volunteer dates ("I had breast cancer 5 years ago"), often follows up with "but I'm clear now."
Product fit Depends on dates. <24 months: HI knocks out (24-month look-back on cancer). HHC + Rx still fits cleanly. >24 months and stable: Both products available. HI may have rider restrictions but base policy fits.
Knockouts to watch This is the classic mid-call DQ scenario. Practice the graceful break (Module 1 §3 · Mid-call DQ) for HI when it fires. Keep HHC alive.
What resonates They know what serious health crises cost. They get the value. They're often grateful someone is offering protection — many of them tried to buy ancillary AFTER their cancer diagnosis and got declined.
Expected close path If clean on dates: standard Phase 6, often closes well. If HI knocks out: mid-call DQ, pivot to HHC-only, present on its own merits. Many cancer survivors close on HHC-only because they understand the home recovery side viscerally.
Watch out for They may volunteer health information you didn't ask for — don't ignore it for the sake of the sale. Honest DQ now > refund call after underwriting catches it.
Profile 6
The 80-Year-Old with Family Far Away

78–85yo, kids/grandkids in other states or far away, often widowed or with frail spouse. May still be sharp but increasingly isolated.

Tells you'll hear "my kids are in [other state]," "I don't want to be a burden," "I'm not sure what would happen if..." Often hesitates on the family-burden discovery question because they don't have an answer.
Product fit HHC is the centerpiece — they have no family safety net for daily-living help. HI is good but smaller value (age-banded commission steps down at 80; rate is 21.67% Y1).
Knockouts to watch Age 85 is HI's outside limit. Mobility issues that already exist may push them toward HHC knockouts. Cognitive decline is more common — confirm cleanly.
What resonates The HHC scenario of staying home vs. being moved to a facility. The TCare cash that lets distant family pay for travel/time off when needed. The fact that this gives them control over what happens if they need help.
Expected close path Slower pace, more compassion. Phase 6 with longer transition. Often closes because the alternative (facility) is the thing they fear most.
Watch out for Cognitive sharpness varies day to day. Make sure they're tracking the conversation — don't rush. If you're unsure they understand the cash flow, slow down and walk through it again. Honest comprehension > rushed sale.
Profile 7
The Medigap Holder

65–75yo, has Medicare Supplement (Medigap) instead of MA, called about something specific — Part D, benefit review, or curiosity.

Tells you'll hear mentions "supplement" or "Medigap" or specific Plan letter (Plan G, Plan F, etc.). May have called because their broker retired or premiums went up.
Product fit Concede HI upfront (Medigap covers hospital copays already; HI is redundant). HHC + Rx still fits cleanly.
Knockouts to watch Medigap holders are often older and may have age-related complications. Confirm HHC knockouts carefully.
What resonates The "I'm not going to sell you something redundant" honesty (massive trust deposit). Then the HHC home-care gap is what they need — Medigap doesn't fill it either. The Rx reimbursement is a clean sweetener.
Expected close path Modified Phase 6 — skip HI entirely. Use the Medigap pre-emptive reframe (Section 11): "Your Medigap is great for what it does — pays the deductibles and coinsurance Medicare leaves on hospital care. You don't need Hospital Indemnity on top of that. But Medigap can't add new benefit categories, so the home care gap is still there. Let me show you Home Healthcare on its own." Then HHC pitch. Then close.
Watch out for They're often skeptical of cross-sell because their broker may have soft-pitched things in the past. The honesty of conceding HI is what earns the conversation about HHC. Don't try to pitch HI anyway — it'll feel pushy and you'll lose the HHC sale too.

  • • "My husband passed" / grief tone → Profile 2
  • • "We're healthy" / spouse-on-call → Profile 3
  • • References to caring for sick spouse → Profile 4

Module 2 · Section 11 · Advanced pushback

Advanced pushback — pre-emptive reframes · 5 advanced objections · emotional curveballs · rapport repair

Module 1 §7 covered the six objections you hear weekly. This section covers the ones that rattle agents because they're rarer — and the emotional moments where sales mode is the wrong mode entirely. Read this section twice. Then come back to it after your first ten calls when one of these lands.

Pre-emptive reframes — use BEFORE the objection surfaces

For prospects who say they have Medigap:

"Your Medigap is great for what it does — it pays the deductibles and coinsurance Medicare leaves on hospital and outpatient care. You don't need Hospital Indemnity on top of that — I'm not going to sell you something redundant. But here's the thing — Medigap can't add new benefit categories. So the home care gap where Medicare won't pay for daily-living help — bathing, dressing, 24-hour help — Medigap doesn't fill that either. There's a prescription reimbursement built into it that usually offsets most of the premium. Want me to walk you through that piece?"
Why this works: The "concede HI upfront" move builds massive trust. Medigap holder closes on HHC alone; refund risk eliminated.

For prospects who say "my MA plan has supplemental benefits":

"Some MA plans do — it's only about 7% of individual plans in 2026, more for special needs plans. And when they do, it's usually capped and limited to chronically ill members. Worth checking your plan's Summary of Benefits — I can walk through it with you. The home care coverage we're discussing pays you cash directly, regardless of what your plan does."
Why this works: The 7% stat (KFF 2026) gives you defensibility. The SOB walk-through offer converts objection into discovery moment.

For prospects who say "I'm in good shape, I won't need this":

"Honestly, that's the right time to set this up. About 70% of adults reaching 65 develop a serious need for help at home, and 20% need care for 5+ years. The thing is, you can only buy this kind of coverage when you're healthy — the underwriting gets stricter once a health change happens. Locking it in now keeps your options open."
Why this works: 70%/20% stats from HHS ASPE — defensible.

For prospects who say "doesn't Medicare cover the first 100 days of home care?":

"Common mix-up — the 100-day rule is for Skilled Nursing Facility after a hospital stay, not home health. Medicare home health has no time limit — your doctor can recertify every 60 days. The catch is what it covers: skilled care like nursing visits or therapy. It doesn't cover help with bathing, dressing, or 24-hour care when those are the only things you need. That's the gap."

5 advanced objections — beyond the top 6

These five show up weekly, not daily. The pattern across all five: acknowledge without agreeing, ask one specific question, hand the prospect back the floor. Never argue. Never try to "overcome." The objections that close are the ones the prospect talks themselves through with you guiding the questions.

Advanced Objection 1
"My financial advisor said no."
"That's smart to run it by your advisor. Did your advisor look at the home-care exposure piece specifically, or was it more of a general 'do you need more insurance' question?"
Why this worksMost advisors give a blanket no without reviewing the specific gap. Surfacing what they actually reviewed reveals whether the no is informed or reflexive.
Advanced Objection 2
"I had a bad experience with insurance."
"I hear that a lot. Most of the time it was either a claim that got denied, or an agent that sold them something they didn't actually need. Which one was it for you?"
Why this worksAcknowledges the wound; asks them to specify. Becomes part of the rapport. Pitching over distrust hardens it.
Advanced Objection 3
"Call me back in 6 months."
"Honest answer — when people say 'call me back in six months,' it usually means either the timing is bad today, or it just isn't a fit. Which is it for you? Either way I want to give you straight info."
Why this worksSurfaces the real objection without judgment. Often produces "it's not a fit" → graceful close, or "today is bad" → real reschedule.
Advanced Objection 4
"My kids handle my insurance."
"Got it. Is there a specific son or daughter who handles it, or is it a family decision? I ask because it sometimes helps if I can give you a one-page summary you can share with them."
Why this worksHonors the deferral, identifies the real decision-maker, offers a take-home that makes the prospect look good to their family.
Advanced Objection 5
"My doctor didn't mention this to me."
"Most doctors don't — they're focused on your medical care, not your insurance gaps. This isn't a medical product, it's a financial-protection product. Your doctor's silence on it is actually normal."
Why this worksReframes the doctor's silence as expected, not red flag. Separates medical authority from insurance authority without challenging the doctor.

3 emotional curveballs — when sales-mode is the wrong mode

Senior calls touch grief, fear, and loss more than any sales category. A prospect might mention a sick spouse, a recent death, or a financial fear that breaks them open mid-call. The agent who treats these moments as obstacles loses the call. The agent who treats them as invitations to be human earns it.

Type 1 · Crying / breaking down
Often triggered by sick spouse, recent loss, or sudden awareness of financial exposure.
"Take a moment. — We can absolutely come back to this another day if today isn't right."
Why this worksPause the call. Don't move forward until they invite it. The offer to reschedule is genuine — some will accept; some will say "no, please keep going."
Type 2 · Venting
About Medicare, the system, prior agents, life in general.
[Listen. Don't redirect. After they finish:] "I hear you. That sounds exhausting. Want me to keep going with this, or is today not the day?"
Why this worksVenting is rapport currency they're giving you. Cutting it short costs more than the time it saves. The check-in after honors what they shared.
Type 3 · Recent loss
Spouse, child, parent — often shared in discovery as context.
"I'm so sorry. — Some people in your shoes find this helpful because it's one less thing to worry about. Others find it's too much on top of everything else. Which side are you on right now?"
Why this worksDirect acknowledgment, two seconds of silence, then the "which side" question lets them choose without pressure.
Sales-mode and grief-mode are mutually exclusive. Switch fully or not at all. The agent who half-acknowledges the moment and then pivots back to pitch is worse than the agent who never acknowledged it. If you're going to be human, be all the way human.

3 rapport repair scripts — when you break it accidentally

Every cross-sell call has moments where you break rapport — rushing, asking something off-tone, talking over them. The agents who close the most are the ones who repair it on the spot. The shared move across all three: acknowledge → make space → invite them back in. Never explain why you did it.

Repair A · Sounded pushy
You hear yourself rushing the close, or feel the prospect pull back. Catch yourself.
"I just realized I was steamrolling you. Let me back up — what's actually on your mind?"
Why this worksSelf-acknowledgment without grovelling. The agent who can name the mistake mid-call earns more trust than the agent who pretends it didn't happen.
Repair B · Asked something wrong
You asked a question that landed weird — too personal, too clinical, off-tone.
"That was probably a weirder question than I intended. Let me ask it differently."
Why this worksShort, light, no apology spiral. You're modeling that mistakes are normal — which makes the prospect more comfortable, not less.
Repair C · Talked over them
You interrupted mid-sentence. The prospect's energy dropped.
"Sorry — you were saying something. Please finish."
Why this worksSeven words. The cleanest repair you'll do all day. Then SHUT UP and actually let them finish — don't fill if they take three seconds to restart.
All three repairs are short. Long apologies make the moment about the agent. Short acknowledgments make it about the prospect. The fastest repair is always the best repair.

The skeptical-of-insurance prospect — a macro trust-building pattern

Some prospects don't object to this product — they object to insurance as a category. They've been oversold before. They watched a relative get burned. They view every agent as a salesperson first and a human second. You can't win this in a single move. You win it across the whole call.

This is different from the "I had a bad experience with insurance" objection — that's a moment to handle. This is a MODE that runs the entire call. When you can hear it in the first 30 seconds, you shift into this pattern.

Three components, in order:

  1. 1. Don't argue. Skepticism gets stronger when you push against it. Acknowledge they have reason to be skeptical: "Most insurance products are oversold. Fair to be cautious."
  2. 2. Earn micro-trust early — give them one piece of straight information that costs you something. Example: "Most ancillary policies are oversold to people who don't really need them. Let me show you when this one makes sense and when it doesn't." The willingness to talk them out of it builds more trust than any benefit list could.
  3. 3. Let them ask. Skeptics respond to inquiry, not pitch. Once trust starts forming, stop describing benefits and ask: "What would make this worth even considering?" Their answer tells you exactly what to say next.
Skeptics close at higher rates than enthusiasts — once you earn their trust. Enthusiasts are easy to start with and easy to lose at the end. Skeptics are hard to start with and hard to lose at the end. The mistake is trying to overcome skepticism with enthusiasm. Match calm with calm. Inquiry with inquiry.

Time pressure — "I only have 5 minutes"

"I only have five minutes" is sometimes literal and sometimes a test. The difference is the prospect's tone and context. Read it before you choose your move.

Path A · Respect it
They sound genuinely rushed. Background noise. Kids. Work in the background.
"Got it. Let me make these five minutes count. The fastest path is for me to ask you two questions, give you a yes/no on fit, and either book a longer call or wrap it up. Sound okay?"
Why this worksCompressed discovery, honest framing, clear exit. You earn the next call by respecting this one. Run the most compressed version of NEPQ you can — two questions, fit verdict, next step.
Path B · Earn more
They sound calm and focused. The "five minutes" sounds like a boundary test, not a real constraint.
"Five is tight for what we'd cover. Honest question — is the five-minute limit firm, or if I made it valuable in the first three would you give me more?"
Why this worksCalls the test without being rude. Most prospects who set artificial limits will extend if you respect them enough to ask. The ones who don't were never going to convert anyway.
The fork is in their voice, not your script. Calm + focused = Path B (earn more). Rushed + scattered = Path A (respect it). Misreading the signal — running Path A on a calm prospect, or Path B on a rushed one — loses the call. Listen for 3 seconds before you pick.

What the master agent did

Earned a $160 (HHC) + $112 (HI) = ~$272 take-home cross-sell on top of the MA close, in 20 minutes added to a 25-minute call. Two clean products. Zero SOA scope-creep. Customer left feeling protected, not sold.

The three moves to copy

  • "Last thing before I let you go" — leverages peak trust right when the customer thinks the call is wrapping. Frames the ancillary cross-sell as completion, not a separate pitch.
  • "Say more about that" on the Preference Question — extracted the emotional anchor ("I built this house in '92") that powered the entire close. Looping is the most under-practiced NEPQ move; it doubled the close strength here.

Most agents who cross-sell after an MA close lead with "Now I want to tell you about Hospital Indemnity." That sounds like a separate pitch. The master move is "Last thing — your new plan still has gaps" — same products, completely different framing. The first sounds like upselling; the second sounds like completing the protection.

Module 3 · Section 13 · Specialized channel

HHC-specific lead sales — a different lead channel, different rules

CPC runs two lead channels for ancillary. The first you already know: MA leads where you cross-sell HI/HHC after MA closes (Module 1) or pivot to HI/HHC when MA fails (Scenario 4 rescue). The second is the topic of this section: ancillary-only leads. Prospects who came in because of an HHC + Rx ad — not a Medicare ad. Most of Module 1 + Module 2 applies. A few things change.

How to recognize an HHC-specific lead

Three signals tell you the lead is ancillary-only, not MA:

  • • Lead source tag in EnrollHere / lead dialer — the creative they responded to was HHC + Rx focused, not Medicare.
  • • Customer's first sentence — "I saw something about home healthcare" or "I'm calling about the prescription benefit." They named the product, not Medicare.
  • • PTC opt-in path — they filled out a form specifically for HHC. The form pre-screened them on prescriptions, knockouts, fit filters. You have Permission to Contact.

If you're unsure, ask: "Just to make sure I have you in the right place — were you reaching out about Medicare today, or about the home healthcare plan specifically?" Their answer routes the call.

Journey 2 — Ancillary-first inbound

The setup: Customer responded to an HHC + Rx ad. They called in (or you called back — both are inbound from a compliance standpoint, because they initiated by filling the form). They're not a Medicare lead. Compliance rule: never discuss MA on this call. If the customer raises MA, route them to a separate inbound MA call they initiate.

The verbal SOA at call start — ancillary categories only

On MA calls, your SOA covers MA, MAPD, PDP, HI, HHC. On an ancillary-only inbound, your SOA covers only the ancillary categories you might discuss (HI + HHC and any other ancillary products you might mention). It does NOT cover MA. This is a real compliance line.

The opener — different from MA

"Hi [Name], this is [Agent] with Clear Path Coverage. You reached out about the home healthcare plan with the prescription benefit — is now a good time to walk through it?"

No MA carrier confirmation. No TPMO disclaimer (TPMO is MA-scope; this is ancillary). The verbal SOA on this call covers ancillary categories — say it after the permission gate, before discovery.

Discovery — same NEPQ seeds, slightly different framing

The four NEPQ seeds (Module 2 §9) apply — but you can't ask "what's the copay on your MA plan?" because you're not discussing MA. Instead:

Hospital exposure (modified): "Whatever Medicare plan you're on right now — if you got admitted to the hospital, do you have a sense of what your out-of-pocket would be per day?" (They may not know — that's fine. The exposure exists whether they know the exact number or not.)
Home-care surface (same as MA): "Have you ever needed help around the house after an illness — bathing, dressing, meals? Medicare doesn't cover that, by the way."
Family-burden (same as MA): "If you needed daily help for a few months, who'd be stepping in?"

The Rx-Net-Cost Reframe (Module 1 §5), the Preference Question (Module 2 §9), Q1-Q4 close (Module 1 §6), the products (Module 1 §3), the objections (Module 1 §7 + Module 2 §11) — all apply unchanged.

Journey 3 — HHC outbound (TCPA-critical)

The setup: Customer opted in via an intake form. The form pre-screened them on Rx + knockouts + fit filters. You have documented Permission to Contact (PTC). This is the only kind of outbound HHC call CPC makes — TCPA requires one-to-one consent. Never outbound-call without documented PTC; the fine is $500-$1,500 per call.

Our system aims to dial within 60 seconds of form submission. Industry data: calling within 1 minute has 7-9x higher conversion than calling within an hour. Their attention is still on the topic, the form is still on their screen, and they're expecting our call. Match your opener to the actual elapsed time so the call feels real, not scripted.

Opener — immediate callback (~60 seconds out)

"Hi [first name], this is [agent] with Clear Path Coverage. You just reached out about the home healthcare plan with the prescription reimbursement — got your info about 30 seconds ago. Wanted to follow up while you're still thinking about it. Is now a good time?"

Opener — delayed callback (15 min to a few hours)

"Hi [first name], this is [agent] with Clear Path Coverage. Following up — you reached out earlier today about the home healthcare plan with the prescription benefit. Wanted to circle back while it's still fresh. Is now a good time?"

If they say "I don't remember signing up"

"Oh, fair — your information was passed to us through a senior benefits service you reached out to. Sometimes folks click on something and don't think much of it. Either way, you've got a minute — is it okay if I ask a couple quick questions to see if there's anything here that fits for you? If it's not a fit, I'll tell you straight."

If they consent, discovery is faster because the form pre-qualified them. The math works for most of them. Don't drag it out — total call time is typically 8-12 minutes (vs. 25-35 min for MA cross-sell).

★ The compliance reality — MA stays out of the conversation

The hardest moment on an ancillary-only call is when the customer says: "By the way, can you help me with my Medicare too?"

The temptation is to say yes and pivot. You can't. Two reasons:

  1. 1. SOA scope. The SOA you captured at the start of this call covers ancillary categories. It does NOT cover MA. Pivoting to MA without a new SOA is a compliance violation.
  2. 2. Lead-to-product mismatch. The lead source / intake form was ancillary-only. Pivoting to MA mid-call breaks the path the customer opted into.

The routing-out script (verbatim):

"Great question and yes, we absolutely help with Medicare Advantage — but I have to ask you to give us a separate call to do that properly. We can't combine those topics on this call. Here's the number to call: [MA inbound number]. Our Medicare team is great. When you call, just tell them you were talking to me on the ancillary side. They'll take great care of you. Now, while I have you — want to keep going on the Home Healthcare piece we were discussing?"

This works because it (a) honors their interest, (b) keeps you compliant, (c) preserves the active conversation. Document the MA referral in EnrollHere as a separate event with the inbound number you gave them.

What changes from MA cross-sell

  • • The opener — no MA carrier confirmation, no TPMO disclaimer.
  • • The verbal SOA — covers ancillary categories only, not MA.
  • • No Soft Close MA section — there's no MA enrollment phase.

  • • Outbound TCPA — only call PTC-documented leads; verify before dialing.
  • • Call length — typically 8-12 minutes for outbound PTC (vs. 25-35 for MA cross-sell).

Income upside + what mastery looks like

Income math

Each ancillary sale typically earns you ~$195 (range: $91 Plan A → $235 Plan C, depending on tier-match). Realistic target: 2–3 cross-sells/day → $10–12K/month extra income on top of your MA commission.

Renewals stack: 5% of premium every year the policy stays in force. MA pays you upfront only; ancillary pays upfront AND every year the customer keeps it. Y5 income builds dramatically as renewals compound.

The decision to attempt cross-sell adds 3–4 minutes to a call — that's the time to transition, surface the exposure, and offer the conversation. When the prospect engages, the full cross-sell cycle (discovery + presentation + close + application + signature) adds 15–20 minutes through to submission. The 3–4 minute attempt is what's "free" — and the close that follows pays it back many times over.

At 15 attempts/day × 15% attach rate × $195/sale = ~$440/day, or ~$60 of commission per minute of attempt time invested. The attempt is the cheapest 3–4 minutes you'll spend all day. Skipping the attempt costs you that math, every time.
